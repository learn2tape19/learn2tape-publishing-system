## Purpose

This document defines exact production specifications for every content format that expresses Learn2Tape's curriculum.

Every derivative (carousel, email, social post, etc.) must preserve the learning objective, belief shift, and clinical conclusion of the canonical blog article while adapting the format for its platform.

## Core Principle: One Learning Objective, Many Formats

Every derivative must:
- Teach the same learning objective
- Express the same belief shift
- Reach the same clinical conclusion
- Preserve evidence boundaries
- Use the appropriate educational CTA for its funnel stage

Platform adaptation may change:
- Length (blog: 1,800 words; email: 400 words)
- Framing (LinkedIn: professional; Instagram: visual)
- Format (blog: narrative; carousel: visual steps)
- Tone intensity (Facebook: conversational; LinkedIn: research-forward)

Platform adaptation may NOT change:
- The underlying clinical lesson
- Safety or contraindication information
- Evidence attribution or accuracy
- The voice (mentor, not instructor)

## CANONICAL BLOG ARTICLE

### Purpose

The canonical blog article is the primary educational expression of each learning objective. All derivatives flow from it.

### Length

- **Minimum:** 700 words
- **Maximum:** 1,200 words
- **Ideal:** ~800 words
- **Exception:** Up to 1,200 words only when the topic genuinely demands additional depth (rare)

**Philosophy:** Long enough to teach one meaningful lesson. Short enough to read over coffee (5-minute read).

Online readers scan, pause, and return later. Shorter, focused articles respect that behavior and increase completion rates. Readers finishing 12 focused articles build more trust than abandoning 12 longer essays. The depth is reserved for the certification course, not the blog.

### Structure (Required)

Every canonical article MUST follow this exact structure:

#### 1. Opening / The Situation (150–300 words)

**Purpose:** Establish the clinical context and make readers recognize their own experience.

**What to do:**
- Start with a specific situation readers encounter in practice
- Make it clear this article addresses something they actually deal with
- Set up the clinical question being answered
- Avoid starting with the answer; start with the problem

**Example opening:**
"You have a patient who comes in with ankle pain after a twist. You assess and find intact ligaments, reasonable ROM, but clear pain with weight-bearing. The question isn't whether to tape—it's how to tape in a way that actually improves function."

#### 2. Common Mistake (200–400 words)

**Purpose:** Help readers recognize patterns they've observed or participated in.

**What to do:**
- Describe what most therapists do (or did)
- Explain why they do it (it makes intuitive sense, or they were taught it that way)
- Show where this approach falls short
- Don't shame; explain why the mistake is understandable

**Example:**
"The most common approach is to tape for stability—ankle in slight inversion, creating an artificial brace. It feels right: you're protecting the ligament. In practice, though, this creates problems..."

#### 3. Better Approach (400–800 words)

**Purpose:** Teach the actual technique, framework, or thinking.

**What to do:**
- Describe the approach in concrete, actionable steps
- Use subheadings to break up content
- Include "how to do it" elements
- Provide decision criteria (when to use, when not to)
- Show examples or variations
- Anticipate "how do I actually do this?" questions

**Example structure:**
- What to assess
- What you're looking for
- How to interpret findings
- How to apply the approach
- What to watch for
- How to know if it's working

#### 4. Why It Works (200–400 words)

**Purpose:** Explain the clinical reasoning so readers understand and can adapt.

**What to do:**
- Explain the biomechanics or clinical principle
- Connect to evidence if relevant
- Explain what changes for the patient
- Help readers understand when this might NOT work
- Teach the principle, not just the technique

**Example:**
"This works because [biomechanical reasoning]. The research shows [specific finding]. In practice, we see [outcome]. This approach is superior in these scenarios: [specific situations]."

#### 5. Clinical Takeaway (100–200 words)

**Purpose:** Crystallize what readers should remember.

**What to do:**
- State the core principle in one sentence
- Summarize key decision criteria
- Give one actionable reminder
- Make it memorable and quotable

**Example:**
"Remember: [Core principle]. The decision point is: [specific assessment]. If [criterion], use [approach]."

#### 6. Continue Learning (50–150 words)

**Purpose:** Create connection to other content and next steps.

**What to do:**
- Link to related Learn2Tape content (2–3 internal links)
- Suggest how to deepen knowledge
- Offer resources or next steps
- Make the path forward clear

**Example:**
"If you want to deepen your understanding of [related topic], read [article link]. The next step is practicing [skill] in your next sessions. Track [specific outcome] to see how this changes your results."

### Formatting Standards

- **Headings:** Use H2 for section headers (## The Situation, ## Common Mistake, etc.)
- **Subheadings:** Use H3 for subsections within main sections
- **Lists:** Use bullet points for clarity; numbered lists for step-by-step processes
- **Emphasis:** Use **bold** for key concepts, *italic* for clinical terms introduced
- **Line breaks:** Add white space between sections for readability
- **No formatting tricks:** Avoid excessive caps, excessive punctuation, or emoji

### Featured Image

- **Dimensions:** 1200 × 628 pixels
- **Format:** PNG or JPG
- **File size:** Optimized for web (< 200 KB)
- **Content:** Illustrates the article topic (clinician thinking, not just hands-on technique)
- **Alt text:** Descriptive, includes keyword naturally (e.g., "Therapist assessing ankle movement quality during clinical reasoning")
- **File naming:** `LO##_TOPIC_FEATURED_v1.0.png`

### SEO Standards

**Page Title (H1)**
- Include primary keyword naturally
- Make it clear and descriptive
- Example: "How to Assess Before You Tape: A Clinical Framework"
- Length: 50–60 characters

**URL Slug**
- Lowercase, hyphens only, no underscores
- Example: `/how-to-assess-before-taping/`
- 50–60 characters max

**Meta Description**
- 150–160 characters
- Summarize the article benefit
- Include primary keyword naturally
- Make it compelling enough to click

**Internal Links**
- Include 2–3 links to related Learn2Tape articles
- Use descriptive anchor text (not "click here")
- Link when genuinely helpful, not forced

**Image Alt Text**
- Describe what's in the image
- Include keyword if it fits naturally
- Make it useful for screen readers
- Example: "Three-step assessment process for ankle injuries"

### Citation and Evidence Standards

**When to Cite**
- Any factual claim about research (cite the source)
- Any statistic or data point
- Any quoted material
- Clinical findings from established research

**How to Cite**
- Use parenthetical citations: (Author, Year)
- Example: "Research shows assessment-driven care improves outcomes by 40% (Smith & Jones, 2023)."
- Provide full citations in a References section at the end if used frequently

**What NOT to Cite**
- Personal clinical observation doesn't need citation
- Commonly known clinical facts don't need citation
- General professional experience doesn't need citation

## INSTAGRAM CAROUSEL

### Purpose

Visual teaching across 5 slides. Hook engagement on Instagram while teaching the learning objective.

### Specifications

- **Format:** PNG or JPG
- **Dimensions:** 1080 × 1350 pixels each slide
- **File naming:** `LO##_TOPIC_CAROUSEL_SLIDE_01_v1.0.png`
- **Quantity:** Exactly 5 slides

### Structure (Required)

#### Slide 1: Hook (Engagement slide)

**Purpose:** Stop scrolling.

**What to include:**
- Bold visual or compelling text
- The core question or insight
- Something that makes readers want to see slides 2–4

**Example:**
"Most therapists tape the wrong way. Here's why."

#### Slides 2–4: Teaching (Three instructional slides)

**Purpose:** Teach the learning objective in digestible pieces.

**What to include:**
- One concept per slide
- Visual examples if possible
- Clear, concise text (20–40 words per slide max)
- Progression: builds from slide 2 → 3 → 4

**Example progression:**
- Slide 2: "The problem most therapists make..."
- Slide 3: "Here's why this matters..."
- Slide 4: "Here's the better approach..."

#### Slide 5: CTA (Call to action)

**Purpose:** Direct engagement or learning.

**What to include:**
- Educational next step (not sales pitch)
- Link to blog (if available)
- Question for comments
- Assessment challenge

**Example:**
"Try this on your next patient. What do you notice?"

### Design Standards

**Text**
- Maximum 40 words per slide
- Large, readable font (minimum 24pt for body text)
- High contrast (dark text on light background or vice versa)
- No crowded or overlapping text

**Images**
- Clinical, professional, authentic
- Show assessment, not just technique
- Real clinic environments, real hands, real clinicians
- Never stock photos or posed images

**Colors**
- Consistent throughout carousel
- Professional, not trendy
- Follow Learn2Tape brand if established
- High contrast for readability

**No Claims Without Support**
- Don't claim "100% success" or "guaranteed results"
- Don't make outcome promises you can't substantiate
- Stick to clinical facts and teaching

### Caption Standards

**Length:** 150 characters max

**Content:**
- Hook the reader
- Mention the learning objective
- Invite engagement (question, reflection, or action)
- Include hashtags (5–7 total)

**Example:**
"Most therapists skip this crucial assessment step. But it changes everything. 🔍 Try this approach with your next patient—tell us what you notice. #ClinicalReasoning #TherapyTips"

### Hashtag Strategy

**Always include:**
- #Learn2Tape
- Brand hashtag if applicable

**Relevant hashtags (3–5 additional):**
- Clinical profession tags (#LicensedMassageTherapist, #PhysicalTherapy, etc.)
- Topic-specific tags (#ClinicalReasoning, #TapingTechnique, etc.)
- Community tags (#TherapyCircle, #BodyworkProfessionals, etc.)

## FACEBOOK POST

### Purpose

Narrative, conversational teaching. Longer form than carousel, but shorter than blog.

### Length

- **Minimum:** 400 words
- **Maximum:** 1,000 words
- **Ideal:** 600–800 words

### Tone

- Conversational, not academic
- Mentor voice (mentor to peer, not instructor to student)
- Direct, approachable, human
- Match the mentor tone from blog (but condensed)

### Structure

**Option A: Abbreviated Blog Structure**
- The Situation (1–2 paragraphs)
- Common Mistake (1 paragraph)
- Better Approach (2–3 paragraphs)
- CTA (1 paragraph)

**Option B: Narrative Structure**
- Open with a story or question
- Teach the approach
- Explain why it matters
- End with CTA and link to blog

### Featured Image

- **Dimensions:** 1200 × 628 pixels (Facebook standard)
- **Content:** Same as blog featured image or a variation
- **File naming:** `LO##_TOPIC_FACEBOOK_v1.0.png`

### CTA (Call to Action)

- **Type:** Educational next step (not sales)
- **Example:** "Read the full article [blog link] for step-by-step details" or "Try this approach and tell us what you observe"
- **Never:** "Buy now" or "Enroll today" (too early in funnel)

### Engagement Strategy

- Ask a question at the end to prompt comments
- Encourage clinicians to share their experience
- Respond to comments with thoughtful replies
- Example: "Have you tried this approach? What did you notice?"

## LINKEDIN ARTICLE/POST

### Purpose

Research-forward, professional positioning. Establish clinical authority.

### Length

- **Minimum:** 800 words
- **Maximum:** 1,500 words
- **Ideal:** 1,000–1,200 words

### Tone

- Professional, authoritative, research-aware
- Mentor voice (but more formal than Facebook)
- Business context (how this improves practice, outcomes, or reputation)
- Avoid consumer-marketing language

### Structure

- **Opening:** Professional hook or research finding
- **Problem:** Why this matters to the profession
- **Solution:** The approach or thinking
- **Evidence:** Research or clinical data supporting it
- **Application:** How to implement in practice
- **Professional Benefit:** How this strengthens practice or authority
- **CTA:** Thought leadership invitation or link to blog

### Featured Image

- **Dimensions:** 1200 × 628 pixels
- **Content:** Professional, clinical setting
- **File naming:** `LO##_TOPIC_LINKEDIN_v1.0.png`

### Professional Positioning

This isn't about pushing a course. This is about establishing you as a clinical leader who thinks deeply about the profession.

**Example angles:**
- "Here's what the research shows that most practitioners miss..."
- "Three ways clinicians can strengthen their authority through better assessment..."
- "Why clinical reasoning separates good practices from great ones..."

## INSTAGRAM STORY

### Purpose

Quick teaser. Engagement and traffic to blog.

### Specifications

- **Format:** PNG or JPG
- **Dimensions:** 1080 × 1920 pixels each frame
- **File naming:** `LO##_TOPIC_STORY_01_v1.0.png`
- **Quantity:** 3–5 frames

### Structure

**Frame 1: Hook**
- Bold text or visual
- Question or insight
- "Tap to read more..."

**Frames 2–3: Quick Teaching**
- One concept per frame
- Visual or text-based
- Keep it simple

**Frame 4–5: CTA**
- Poll or question for engagement
- "Link in bio for full article"
- Call to action (comment, share, visit blog)

### Specifications

- **Text:** Large, readable (minimum 28pt)
- **Visuals:** Clinical, professional, authentic
- **Duration:** 24-hour story (auto-delete)
- **Frequency:** 1–2 stories per blog publication

## NEWSLETTER EMAIL

### Purpose

Drive traffic to blog. Nurture email list with educational content.

### Length

- **Minimum:** 300 words
- **Maximum:** 600 words
- **Ideal:** 400–500 words

Shorter emails feel incomplete. Longer emails lose attention. The goal is to educate enough that readers want to read the full blog.

### Structure

**Subject Line**
- [Clinical question] + [promise]
- Example: "Why Your Assessment Matters More Than Your Technique"
- Length: 40–50 characters
- Include keyword if natural

**Preview Text**
- 40–50 characters
- What appears in inbox preview
- Make it compelling enough to open

**Opening Paragraph**
- The Situation (adapted for email)
- Make it relevant to the reader
- Example: "You have a patient with ankle pain. You assess and find..."

**Teaching Paragraph(s)**
- 200–300 words max
- Summarize the key insight from the blog
- Don't reproduce the full blog
- Tease the depth of the full article

**Clinical Takeaway**
- One memorable sentence
- The core principle
- Make it quotable

**CTA**
- Link to full blog article
- Button text: "Read the Full Article" or "Learn More"
- Include blog URL

**Footer**
- Unsubscribe option (required by law)
- Contact information
- Social links (optional)

### Email Copy Standards

- **Tone:** Mentor voice, slightly more formal than carousel
- **Voice:** Same as blog (clinical authority, not sales)
- **Personalization:** Use reader name if available
- **No hype:** No "amazing," "revolutionary," "game-changing"
- **Clinical accuracy:** Preserve all clinical nuance

- **List:** Assigned per Curriculum Delivery Plan

**Examples:**


**Rules:**
- Uppercase learning objective prefix (LO##)
- Topic in ALL_CAPS_WITH_UNDERSCORES
- Platform clearly identified
- Version using vMAJOR.MINOR format
- No spaces, no vague names like "final" or "v2"

## Accessibility Standards

### Alt Text (All Images)

- Describe what's in the image
- Include relevant context
- Minimum 125 characters
- Make it useful for screen readers
- Don't start with "image of" (screen readers already say that)

**Example:**
"Therapist performing ankle movement quality assessment, showing knee flexion position used to evaluate foot dorsiflexion"

### Video Captions (If Applicable)

- Accurate transcription of spoken content
- Include speaker labels if multiple voices
- Note significant pauses or music
- Minimum 99% accuracy

### Color Contrast

- Text on background must have sufficient contrast ratio
- Minimum WCAG AA (4.5:1 for normal text)
- Test using contrast checker tools
