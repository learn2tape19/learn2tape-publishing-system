## Purpose

Establish the step-by-step process for producing Learn2Tape content from idea through publication, distribution, and analytics. This document is the production manual—anyone following it should be able to produce consistent, quality content that meets our standards.

This is a mechanical document. It describes what to do, in order, without interpretation. When you don't know what to do next, you follow this workflow.

---

## Scope

This document governs:
- How ideas enter the publishing system
- How research is conducted and organized
- How content is written and structured
- How content is reviewed and refined
- How content is optimized for publication
- How content is distributed across channels
- How content is tracked and measured
- How results inform future decisions

This document does not govern:
- Whether an idea should be published (see 01_EDITORIAL_CHARTER)
- What topics to publish (see 02_EDITORIAL_CALENDAR)
- Standards for derivative formats (see 05_DERIVATIVE_STANDARDS)

## Authority

This workflow is the binding production process. Deviations from this workflow should be rare and documented in the Decision Log.

Changes to this workflow should only be made when production friction appears (something takes longer than it should, produces unclear results, or misses quality standards).

## Phase 1: Idea Capture

### Where Ideas Come From

Ideas are captured from:
- **Editorial Calendar** — Topics already approved for the quarter (02_EDITORIAL_CALENDAR)
- **Content Backlog** — Ideas documented for future use (07_CONTENT_BACKLOG)
- **Production Gaps** — Identified during research or writing
- **Audience Feedback** — Comments, emails, or direct requests from readers
- **Clinical Observation** — Patterns noticed in practice or training

### Idea Documentation

When an idea is ready to move into production, capture:
- **Topic** — Clear, specific title or description
- **Clinical Problem** — What problem does this article solve?
- **Target Audience** — Which maturity level(s)?
- **Content Pillar** — Assessment, Taping, or Professional Practice?
- **Week/Phase** — Where does it fit in the editorial calendar?
- **Primary CTA** — What do you want readers to do or learn?
- **Estimated Length** — Article word count expectation
- **Key Sources** — Initial research references or experience base
- **Dependencies** — Does this build on another article?

### Gate: Editorial Approval

Before moving to research, the idea must be approved by Drew Freedman:
- Does it align with the editorial calendar?
- Does it pass the editorial decision framework (01_EDITORIAL_CHARTER)?
- Is this the right time to publish it?
- Do we have expertise to teach this?

**If approved:** Move to Phase 2 (Research)
**If rejected:** Document in Content Backlog with reason; revisit later or abandon

## Phase 2: Research & Planning

### Research Process

Conduct research in this order:

#### Step 1: Identify the Knowledge Base
- What do I already know from clinical experience?
- What have I already taught others?
- What patterns have I observed?
- What am I confident about?

#### Step 2: Review Existing Learn2Tape Content
- Does Learn2Tape have existing content on this?
- How does this article relate to existing content?
- Does this contradict, build on, or introduce new material?
- What connections should we make?

#### Step 3: Collect Evidence
For the specific clinical claim:
- What research exists? (peer-reviewed, published, preprint)
- What does the research actually say? (not what we wish it said)
- How applicable is this research to our audience?
- What are the limitations of the research?

#### Step 4: Identify Real-World Applications
- Where have I seen this work in practice?
- Where have I seen this fail?
- What populations does it work best for?
- What populations should avoid it?
- What are common implementation mistakes?

#### Step 5: Anticipate Reader Questions
- What will clinicians want to know?
- What confusions will arise?
- What contradictions need addressing?
- What next steps should we suggest?

### Planning Document

Create a simple planning document (doesn't need to be formatted; used internally only):

```
ARTICLE PLAN: [Title]

Clinical Problem:
[One sentence: what problem are we solving?]

Key Points (3-5):
- [Main teaching point]
- [Main teaching point]
- [Main teaching point]

Evidence:
- [Source 1 - what it shows]
- [Source 2 - what it shows]
- Clinical experience: [observations]

Real-World Applications:
- Works well when: [specific scenario]
- Doesn't work when: [specific scenario]
- Common mistake: [specific mistake]

Reader Questions We'll Address:
- How is this different from [related approach]?
- When should I use this vs. [alternative]?
- What if [common objection]?

Structure:
- Situation: [context readers recognize]
- Common Mistake: [what most people do wrong]
- Better Approach: [what we're teaching]
- Why It Works: [the reasoning]
- Clinical Takeaway: [what to remember]
- Continue Learning: [next steps]

Primary CTA:
[What do we want readers to do?]

Word Count Target: [number]
```

### Gate: Research Completion

Before moving to drafting, confirm:
- Do you have enough information to write?
- Are your clinical positions defensible?
- Have you identified potential contradictions?
- Do you know who this article is for?

## Phase 3: Drafting

### Structural Framework

Every article follows this structure. This is not flexible:

#### 1. Opening / Situation (150-300 words)
**Purpose:** Establish the clinical context and make readers recognize their own experience.

- Start with a specific situation readers encounter in practice
- Make it clear this article is about something they actually deal with
- Set up the clinical question we're answering
- Don't start with the answer; start with the problem

**Example:**
"You have a patient who comes in with ankle pain after a twist. You assess and find intact ligaments, reasonable ROM, but clear pain with weight-bearing. The question isn't whether to tape—it's how to tape in a way that actually improves function. Most therapists miss a critical assessment step here..."

#### 2. Common Mistake (200-400 words)
**Purpose:** Help readers recognize patterns they've observed or participated in.

- Describe what most therapists do
- Explain why they do it (it makes intuitive sense, or they were taught it that way, or it partially works)
- Show where this approach falls short
- Don't shame; explain why the mistake is understandable

**Example:**
"The most common approach is to tape for stability—ankle in slight inversion, creating an artificial brace. It feels right: you're protecting the ligament. In practice, though, we've found this creates problems..."

#### 3. Better Approach (400-800 words)
**Purpose:** Teach the actual technique or thinking.

- Describe the approach in concrete, actionable steps
- Use subheadings to break up sections
- Include "how to" elements
- Provide decision criteria (when to use this, when not to)
- Show examples or variations
- Anticipate "how do I actually do this?" questions

**Example:**
"Here's a different starting position that we've found more effective..."
[Include specific assessment criteria, step-by-step technique, what you're assessing for, how to know if you're doing it right]

#### 4. Why It Works (200-400 words)
**Purpose:** Explain the clinical reasoning so readers understand and can adapt.

- Explain the biomechanics
- Connect to evidence if relevant
- Explain what changes for the patient
- Help readers understand when this might not work
- Teach the principle, not just the technique

**Example:**
"This works because [biomechanical reasoning]. The research shows [specific finding]. In practice, we see [outcome]. This approach is superior in these scenarios: [specific situations]."

#### 5. Clinical Takeaway (100-200 words)
**Purpose:** Crystallize what readers should remember.

- State the core principle in one sentence
- Summarize the key decision criteria
- Give one actionable reminder
- Make it memorable

**Example:**
"Remember: [Core principle]. The decision point is: [specific assessment]. If [criterion], use [approach]."

#### 6. Continue Learning (50-150 words)
**Purpose:** Create connection to other content and next steps.

- Link to related Learn2Tape content
- Suggest how to deepen knowledge
- Offer resources or next steps
- Make the path forward clear

**Example:**
"If you want to deepen your understanding of [related topic], read [article link]. The next step is practicing [skill] in your next sessions. Track [specific outcome] to see how this changes your results."

### Drafting Process

1. **Write without editing** — Get the ideas down first. Don't perfect sentences yet.
2. **Follow the structure** — Use the sections above even if it feels repetitive.
3. **Write for clarity, not impressiveness** — Simple, direct language always wins.
4. **Anticipate questions** — Add explanation for anything that might confuse readers.
5. **Include specifics** — Use real examples, real techniques, real scenarios.
6. **Write conversationally** — Pretend you're teaching a colleague.
7. **Keep your own voice** — Don't force a fake tone; be yourself but professional.

### Target Length

- **Minimum:** 1,200 words
- **Maximum:** 2,000 words
- **Ideal:** 1,400-1,800 words

(Shorter articles feel incomplete; longer articles lose readers' attention. If it's longer, you have two articles.)

## Phase 4: Clinical Review

### What Gets Reviewed

Your draft is reviewed for:
1. **Clinical accuracy** — Are all factual claims correct?
2. **Safety** — Are contraindications and precautions clear?
3. **Completeness** — Can readers implement this?
4. **Evidence accuracy** — Are citations correct and in context?
5. **Appropriateness** — Is this suitable for our audience?

### Who Reviews

**Primary Reviewer:** Drew Freedman (or designated clinical expert)

### Review Process

1. Read the article in full
2. Evaluate against each criterion above
3. Mark specific issues for revision
4. Document any major concerns or suggestions
5. Provide constructive feedback on clarity and completeness

### What "Passes" Clinical Review

The article passes when:
- All factual claims are accurate
- Contraindications and limitations are clear
- A competent clinician could implement this
- Evidence is accurately represented
- The clinical reasoning is sound

**Result:** Article proceeds to Phase 5 (Voice & Style)

### What "Fails" Clinical Review

The article is rejected if:
- Core claims are clinically invalid
- Safety concerns are not adequately addressed
- The teaching method might lead clinicians astray
- There's insufficient clinical grounding
- It contradicts established Learn2Tape position without justification

**Result:** Article is revised or abandoned. Document in Decision Log if rejected.

## Phase 5: Voice & Style Revision

### What Gets Reviewed

Your article is revised for:
1. **Voice consistency** — Does it sound like Learn2Tape?
2. **Tone appropriateness** — Is it mentor-like, not instructional?
3. **Clarity and concision** — Is every sentence necessary?
4. **Reader experience** — Will readers stay engaged?
5. **Editorial standards** — Does it match our house style?

### Revision Checklist

- [ ] Does it use the Learn2Tape voice? (See 01_EDITORIAL_CHARTER)
- [ ] Does it avoid marketing language?
- [ ] Are there any vague or unclear sentences?
- [ ] Does every section serve a purpose?
- [ ] Are examples specific enough?
- [ ] Does it maintain consistent perspective?
- [ ] Are transitions smooth?
- [ ] Does it end well?

### Who Revises

**Primary Reviser:** Drew Freedman (or designated editor)

### Revision Types

**Level 1 (Minor):** Grammar, word choice, clarity
- Implement automatically

**Level 2 (Moderate):** Tone, emphasis, structure
- Flag for author review, discuss if needed

**Level 3 (Major):** Approach, significant rewriting, substantial changes
- Discuss with author; may require new draft

## Phase 6: SEO Optimization

### SEO Review (Not SEO Hijacking)

SEO optimization serves readers, not search engines. The goal is to help people searching for solutions find our article. We don't optimize for rankings at the expense of clarity or authenticity.

### SEO Elements to Optimize

#### Title
- **What we do:** Make it clear and descriptive
- **What we avoid:** Clickbait, keyword stuffing, false promises
- **Example good:** "How to Assess Before You Tape: A Clinical Framework"
- **Example bad:** "Therapists HATE This One Taping Trick"

#### Meta Description (160 characters)
- Summarize the article benefit
- Include primary keyword naturally
- Make it compelling enough to click
- Example: "Learn the assessment step most therapists skip—it changes your taping outcomes. A clinical framework for better decisions."

#### URL/Slug
- Keep it short and descriptive
- Use hyphens (not underscores)
- Include primary keyword if natural
- Example: `/how-to-assess-before-taping/`

#### Headings
- Use H2 for section headers (we already have these)
- Make sure they're descriptive
- Include keywords when natural
- Don't force keywords; clarity wins

#### Internal Links (2-3 per article)
- Link to related Learn2Tape articles
- Use descriptive anchor text (not "click here")
- Link when it's genuinely helpful, not forced
- Example: "If you want to deepen your understanding of ankle biomechanics, read [Clinical Assessment article]."

#### Featured Image Alt Text
- Describe what's in the image
- Include keyword if it fits naturally
- Make it useful for screen readers
- Example: "Therapist performing ankle assessment, showing knee flexion test"

### SEO Gate

Before publication, confirm:
- [ ] Title is clear and includes primary keyword
- [ ] Meta description is compelling and accurate
- [ ] URL slug is clean and descriptive
- [ ] Headings are descriptive (not keyword-stuffed)
- [ ] 2-3 internal links are included
- [ ] Image alt text describes the image

## Phase 7: Media Preparation

### Featured Image

#### Source Priority
1. **Owned photography** — Shoot new images if needed
2. **Professional stock** — If we own a library
3. **Free licensed** — Only if nothing else available
4. **Never:** Generic stock, posed images, clipart

#### Specifications
- **Dimensions:** 1200 x 628 pixels (web standard)
- **Format:** PNG or JPG
- **Compression:** Optimized for web (< 200KB)
- **Content:** Should illustrate the article topic
- **File naming:** `[Week]_[Topic]_Featured.png`

#### Quality Check
- [ ] Image is clear and professional
- [ ] Image illustrates the article concept
- [ ] Image is oriented correctly
- [ ] Alt text is descriptive
- [ ] File is properly compressed
- [ ] File is named correctly

### In-Article Images (if used)

#### Source and Specifications
- Same requirements as featured image
- Illustrate specific techniques or assessments
- Minimum size: 600px width
- Maximum size: Full-width page width
- File naming: `[Week]_[Topic]_[Description]_[#].png`

#### Permissions
- Confirm all subjects have consented to publication
- Ensure clinical privacy (faces visible only with consent, HIPAA compliance)
- Document consent in asset library

## Phase 12: Learning & Decision Log

### Lessons Capture

After 4 weeks of an article being live, capture:

**What Worked Well**
- What aspect of this article resonated with readers?
- What drove the most traffic?
- What generated positive feedback?
- What CTA converted best?

**What Could Improve**
- What sections got skipped (based on scroll depth)?
- What questions came up that the article didn't answer?
- What social platforms did it not perform on?
- What email subject line got lower open rate?

**What to Do Differently Next Time**
- More/less of this topic?
- Different structure or format?
- Different CTA?
- Different promotion strategy?

### Decision Log

Most articles don't warrant Decision Log entry (they just inform ongoing patterns).

**Decision Log Entry Required When:**
- We make a material change to the workflow based on this article
- We discover a significant gap in our system
- We learn something that contradicts prior assumptions
- We need to remember WHY we made a specific choice

## Workflow Checkpoints & Gates

Every phase of this workflow has a gate. If an article doesn't pass, it either gets revised or rejected.

| Phase | Gate | Pass Criteria | Fail Action |
|-------|------|---------------|-------------|
| Idea Capture | Editorial Approval | Aligns with calendar + passes editorial framework | Reject or backlog |
| Research | Completion | Sufficient knowledge to write confidently | Extend research or abandon |
| Drafting | Structure Compliance | Follows the required structure | Revise to structure |
| Clinical Review | Accuracy + Safety | All claims accurate, safety clear, implementation possible | Revise or reject |
| Voice & Style | Brand Consistency | Sounds like Learn2Tape, mentor tone, clear | Revise |
| SEO | Optimization | Title, meta, links, keywords all optimized | Adjust SEO elements |
| Media | Quality | Images professional, alt text complete, sizing correct | Replace images or remove |
| Derivatives | Format Compliance | Each derivative meets platform standards | Recreate to standards |
| Scheduling | Pre-pub Checklist | All boxes checked before live | Don't publish; finish items |
| Distribution | Live Verification | Article live, links working, email sent | Fix issues before distributing |

## Workflow Flexibility

### When to Deviate

This workflow should be followed consistently. Deviations are rare and should be documented.

**Acceptable deviations:**
- Emergency/reactive content (e.g., important safety issue)
- Shorter articles for specific purposes (e.g., quick tip)
- Guest content (follows same workflow but with approval differences)

**Process for deviation:**
1. Document the deviation in Decision Log
2. Document the reason
3. Still try to follow as much of this workflow as possible
4. Use the deviation to identify workflow improvements

## Common Challenges & Solutions

### Challenge: Perfectionism Delays Publishing

**Solution:** Set revision limits. Once you've revised a section twice, it's done. Move forward.

### Challenge: Scope Creep (Article Keeps Growing)

**Solution:** If an article exceeds 2,000 words, you have two articles. Split it and publish separately.

### Challenge: Research Takes Too Long

**Solution:** Set a research deadline. If you don't have enough after 2 weeks, write with what you have and note limitations.

### Challenge: Uncertain About Clinical Accuracy

**Solution:** This is the Clinical Review gate. Resolve doubts there, or don't publish.

### Challenge: Workflow Feels Slow

**Solution:** The timeline is realistic. Trying to rush increases error risk. Follow the workflow.
