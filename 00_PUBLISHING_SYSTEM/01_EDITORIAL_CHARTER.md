## Purpose

Establish the non-negotiable editorial standards that govern every piece of Learn2Tape content. This document defines what we publish, how we publish it, what we refuse to publish, and how editorial decisions are made.

The Editorial Charter is the constitution of Learn2Tape publishing. When editorial judgment is required, this document is the authority.

This document governs:
- Content standards (clinical validity, accuracy, completeness)
- Voice and tone consistency requirements
- Editorial decision-making framework
- Quality gates that content must pass before publication
- Content we will not publish and why
- Conflict of interest and disclosure standards
- Author and sourcing standards

This document does not govern:

## Core Editorial Principles

### 1. Clinical Validity > Everything Else

Every article must reflect what actually works in clinical practice. If research contradicts practice, we investigate the contradiction. If practice contradicts research, we say so and explain why.

**What This Means**
- We don't publish approaches we haven't validated in real clinical settings
- We don't recommend techniques just because they're popular
- We don't avoid clinical nuance to sound simple
- We test thinking against reality before we teach it

**Decision Rule:** If you wouldn't recommend this approach to a therapist treating your family member, you don't publish it.

---

### 2. Evidence Informs Without Dictating

Research matters. Practice experience matters more. When they align, we teach with confidence. When they diverge, we acknowledge both and help readers reconcile them.

**What This Means**
- We cite research when it exists and is relevant
- We're honest about the limits of research (sample size, populations, applicability)
- We weight clinical experience appropriately alongside academic evidence
- We don't hide behind "the research shows" to avoid taking a position
- We teach readers how to evaluate evidence, not just what the evidence says

**Decision Rule:** "The research shows X" is not sufficient reason to teach X if practice shows otherwise. Explain the gap.

---

### 3. Clarity Over Cleverness

The reader's understanding matters more than our wordcraft. We write so that a competent clinician can implement what we teach immediately, not eventually after research or trial-and-error.

**What This Means**
- We define terms the first time we use them
- We use concrete examples over abstract concepts
- We break complex ideas into digestible steps
- We show, don't tell
- We anticipate confusion and address it directly

**Decision Rule:** If a sentence requires re-reading, it needs rewriting.

---

### 4. Honesty About Limitations

Good clinical teaching acknowledges what we don't know and when our approach won't work. Overstating certainty loses trust and wastes readers' time.

**What This Means**
- We state who this approach works best for
- We identify populations or conditions where it doesn't work
- We acknowledge alternative valid approaches
- We admit when we don't have a good answer
- We correct published errors visibly and promptly

**Decision Rule:** "This won't work for X" is more credible than "This works for everyone."

---

### 5. Professional Authority Built on Consistency

Readers trust Learn2Tape because we are reliable. That reliability comes from consistency—in accuracy, in values, in voice, in depth—over time.

**What This Means**
- We don't make editorial exceptions for business reasons
- We maintain the same standards for every article
- We don't publish when we're not sure
- We build patterns readers come to rely on
- We earn authority through repetition of excellence

**Decision Rule:** One weak article undermines three strong ones.

## Content Standards

### Clinical Accuracy

Every article must meet these accuracy standards:

- **Anatomical accuracy** — All references to anatomy are correct and appropriately detailed for the audience
- **Biomechanical accuracy** — Descriptions of movement, force, and tissue response are valid
- **Technique accuracy** — If we teach a technique, it is demonstrated correctly and would work if followed
- **Safety accuracy** — We identify contraindications, precautions, and potential risks
- **Patient population accuracy** — We specify which populations the approach is appropriate for
- **Evidence accuracy** — Any citations are correct, in context, and properly attributed

**Quality Gate:** Clinical review (see 03_CONTENT_WORKFLOW) catches accuracy errors before publication.

### Completeness

Articles should be complete enough to be useful on their own. Readers should not need to purchase a course to understand the article.

**What This Means**
- We provide enough clinical context to matter
- We teach the actual approach, not just the theory
- We address common implementation problems
- We help readers know when and how to apply it
- We offer next steps for deeper learning (not upsells)

**Decision Rule:** A reader who never enrolls should still feel like they learned something valuable.

### Actionability

Abstract knowledge doesn't change practice. Every article should enable readers to actually do something different in their next session.

**What This Means**
- We teach techniques readers can implement immediately
- We provide assessment methods they can use
- We give decision frameworks they can apply
- We show real examples from actual practice
- We anticipate and answer "how do I actually do this?" questions

**Decision Rule:** If a clinician reads your article and then doesn't know how to change their practice, you need to rewrite it.

### Depth Appropriateness

We teach to the level that serves our audience, not deeper or shallower.

**What This Means**
- We assume readers understand anatomy and basic physiology
- We don't oversimplify complex clinical concepts
- We don't require doctoral-level expertise to understand
- We provide depth without requiring specialized background
- We explain our thinking, not just our conclusions

**Decision Rule:** Assume a competent practitioner with 2–3 years of experience. Make it clear for them; don't make them feel stupid.

## Voice & Tone Requirements

### Required Elements

Every article must have:
- **A clinician voice** — written as if from one skilled therapist to another, not from an institution to an audience
- **Evidence of clinical judgment** — the author has thought about this, not just collected information
- **Honest perspective** — the author takes a position, not a neutral stance
- **Patient-centered thinking** — the ultimate goal is better outcomes for the people being treated
- **Professional warmth** — approachable without being casual, authoritative without being distant

### Prohibited Patterns

We never:
- Use manufactured urgency ("Act now!" "Don't miss out!")
- Use empty enthusiasm ("Amazing!" "Incredible!" "Game-changing!")
- Use manipulation ("Most therapists miss this..." when designed to create insecurity)
- Hide behind passive voice to avoid accountability
- Treat readers as objects to be sold to
- Oversell or underestimate difficulty
- Use clickbait headlines that don't match the content
- Adopt corporate-speak or marketing language

### What This Sounds Like

**Good Opens**
- "Here's something I wish someone had taught me twenty years ago."
- "Most therapists make this mistake. Here's why it happens and how to avoid it."
- "This technique works better than people realize, but only if you understand why."
- "You're probably doing this correctly, but these three subtle adjustments will get better results."

**Bad Opens**
- "You won't believe this one trick therapists hate!"
- "Revolutionary new approach changing clinical outcomes!"
- "Try this amazing technique that will transform your practice!"
- "Therapists are making this mistake—and it's costing them money!"

## What We Publish

### Topics We Cover

We publish about:
- **Assessment and clinical reasoning** — How to think about what you're seeing
- **Taping application and technique** — How to apply tape effectively
- **Professional practice** — How to structure your practice and communicate with patients
- **Clinical errors and corrections** — Mistakes we've made and what we learned
- **Evidence synthesis** — What research shows and how to interpret it
- **Patient outcomes** — How to measure what matters clinically

### Audience Relevance

Every article answers one of these questions:
- "How do I do this better?"
- "Why isn't this working?"
- "What should I believe about this?"
- "How do I know if I'm ready for this?"
- "What am I missing about this?"
- "How do I think about this clinical problem?"

**Decision Rule:** If the article doesn't answer one of these questions, it's not ready to publish.

---

## What We Don't Publish

### Hard Boundaries

We do not publish:
- **Generic content** — If it could apply to any profession, we don't publish it
- **Unvalidated approaches** — If we haven't tested it in real clinical settings, we don't teach it
- **Contraindicated practices** — If evidence shows it's harmful, we don't recommend it
- **Speculation without foundation** — If we're just guessing, we don't publish
- **Unattributed claims** — If we can't support it or cite it, we don't say it
- **Competitor attacks** — If it's about another brand or person, we don't publish it
- **Product advertising** — If the real purpose is to sell something, we don't publish it
- **Borrowed authority** — If we're not the expert, we don't claim expertise

**Decision Rule:** When in doubt, don't publish. There's no cost to waiting.

### Topics We Avoid

We avoid—but don't absolutely prohibit—content about:
- **Controversial clinical debates** — Unless we have something genuinely new to add
- **Politics or social issues** — Unless directly clinically relevant (e.g., patient equity)
- **Personal lifestyle content** — Unless it illustrates a clinical principle
- **Continuing education compliance** — We teach mastery, not credits

## Clinical Disclosure Standards

### What Must Be Disclosed

- **Financial interest** — If you have financial interest in a product, treatment, or outcome, disclose it
- **Experiential limitation** — If your experience is narrow (e.g., "I've only done this in sports settings"), disclose it
- **Counterarguments** — If there's a legitimate opposing view, present it
- **Uncertainty** — If you're not certain about something you're teaching, say so
- **Conflict of interest** — If you have reason to be biased, acknowledge it

### What Doesn't Need Disclosure

- The fact that you're promoting your own course (it's obvious)
- Your personal preference for an approach (that's your perspective)
- The fact that you've made mistakes (we all have)

**Decision Rule:** Ask yourself: "Would a clinician want to know this before deciding whether to trust this advice?" If yes, disclose it.

## Editorial Decision Framework

When you're unsure whether to publish, ask in this order:

1. **Is this clinically valid?**
   - Does it work in real practice?
   - Is it safe?
   - Is there evidence to support it?
   - Are the limitations clear?
   - *If no → Don't publish.*

2. **Is this clear enough for readers to understand?**
   - Can a competent clinician implement this from the article?
   - Have we anticipated common confusions?
   - Is the depth appropriate?
   - *If no → Rewrite or don't publish.*

3. **Does this match our voice?**
   - Does it sound like a clinician talking to peers?
   - Is it honest and direct?
   - Does it avoid manipulation?
   - *If no → Revise the voice.*

4. **Is this useful enough to justify publishing?**
   - Will readers feel they learned something?
   - Can they apply it?
   - Does it advance their clinical thinking?
   - *If no → Don't publish.*

5. **Is this aligned with our publishing mission?**
   - Does it improve clinical practice?
   - Does it build professional trust?
   - Does it support our brand position?
   - *If no → Don't publish.*

**Publishing Gate:** Content must pass all five tests before publication. Any "no" is cause for revision or rejection.

## Author Standards

### Who Can Write Learn2Tape Content

Learn2Tape content is written by:
- **Drew Freedman** — Primary voice and final editorial authority
- **Guest authors** — Licensed clinicians with established expertise, approved by Drew Freedman

### Guest Author Standards

If Learn2Tape publishes guest content, the author must:
- Be a licensed practitioner in their field
- Have demonstrated clinical expertise (not just credentials)
- Align with Learn2Tape values and voice
- Submit to editorial review and revision
- Disclose any financial interest in the topic
- Agree to have their work edited to match Learn2Tape standards

### Approval Process

Guest content requires:
1. Pre-approval from Drew Freedman (before writing)
2. Clinical review by Drew Freedman
3. Editorial revision to match voice and standards
4. Final approval from Drew Freedman before publication

## Correction and Accountability

### If We Publish an Error

- We acknowledge it promptly
- We correct it visibly (not silently)
- We explain what was wrong and why
- We describe what's correct now
- We use corrections to improve our process

### What Triggers a Correction

- Factual inaccuracy (anatomy, technique, evidence)
- Clinical advice that could cause harm
- Misrepresentation or misleading language
- Breached ethical standard
- Broken link or invalid reference

### What Doesn't Trigger a Correction

- Opinion someone disagrees with
- A better way to say the same thing
- Updated thinking (we can update articles, not just correct them)
- Typos (fix them, but don't announce a "correction")

## Appendix: Common Editorial Questions & Answers

**Q: Can we publish something that contradicts what we taught six months ago?**
A: Only if our thinking has genuinely evolved and we acknowledge the change. Explain why we believed the first thing, what changed our thinking, and what we believe now.

**Q: Should we publish if it might hurt sales of our course?**
A: Yes, if it's clinically true. Teaching people not to enroll teaches them to trust us. They enroll later if we earn it.

**Q: Can we soften the message if it might offend readers?**
A: No, if the clinical message is accurate. You can be kind in how you deliver it, but don't dilute clinical truth.

**Q: How much time can we spend editing an article?**
A: As long as needed to meet standards. If an article isn't worth substantial editing, it's not worth publishing.

**Q: Should we publish if it's derivative of someone else's work?**
A: Not without attribution and substantial new insight. If you're mostly summarizing, link to the original instead.

**Q: Can we publish purely to hit a publishing schedule?**
A: No. Missing a schedule is better than publishing weak content.

**Q: Should we write about controversial topics?**
A: Only if you have something genuinely valuable to add that goes beyond the existing debate.
