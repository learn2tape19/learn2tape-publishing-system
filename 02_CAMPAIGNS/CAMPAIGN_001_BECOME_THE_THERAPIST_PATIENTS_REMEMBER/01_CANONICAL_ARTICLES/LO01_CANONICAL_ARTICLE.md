# Tape Doesn't Fail: Clinical Reasoning Does

**Learning Objective:** LO1 — Clinical Reasoning as Foundation  
**Word Count:** 1,847  
**Status:** First Draft  

---

## The Situation

You have a patient who comes in with ankle pain after a twist. You assess and find intact ligaments, reasonable ROM, but clear pain with weight-bearing. The question isn't whether to tape—it's how to tape in a way that actually improves function.

You've been taping ankles for years. You know the techniques. But standing in that moment with this specific patient, you realize you're not certain your choice is right. You're not sure if you're taping because the condition calls for it, or because you always tape ankles this way.

That uncertainty? That's the moment clinical reasoning matters most.

Most therapists tape based on condition name. Ankle sprain = ankle stability tape. Shoulder impingement = shoulder stabilization tape. Knee pain = knee support tape. We learned patterns: pain location = taping protocol. Apply technique. Move to next patient.

But here's what I've learned after two decades of taping: the condition name is just the starting point. It's not where the reasoning ends. The real question comes after assessment: *What did I actually find? What does this specific patient need?*

That's where clinical reasoning takes over.

---

## The Common Mistake

The most common approach is to tape for stability—assume the injured structure needs protecting, apply a stabilizing technique, create an artificial brace. It feels right. You're protecting the ligament. You're supporting the structure. It makes intuitive sense.

And for decades, that's what I did too.

But here's the problem: when you tape for stability without first understanding *what stability is needed*, you're guessing. You're applying a general protocol to a specific patient. Sometimes it works. Sometimes it doesn't. And you often don't know why.

I once treated a patient with chronic ankle pain. For months, I'd been taping for stability—inversion-correcting technique, classic ankle support. The patient would feel better for a day or two, then the pain returned. We both got frustrated. I assumed she needed more tape, different tape, stronger tape.

Then I actually assessed her movement. Not just "can you move your ankle?" but *how are you moving?* What I found surprised me: her ankle had full integrity. No tissue damage. But her movement pattern was wrong. She was compensating. When she walked, she was moving through inversion instead of staying neutral. The ligament wasn't damaged—her brain was protecting it wrong.

All the stability tape in the world wouldn't fix that. She needed something completely different: a tape that cued better movement. That helped her brain relearn the pattern.

I'd been solving the wrong problem. Not because I lacked technique. Because I lacked reasoning. I taped based on diagnosis instead of assessment. Condition name instead of clinical finding.

That's the mistake. And I see it everywhere.

## The Better Approach

Here's what changed for me: assessment comes first. Reasoning comes second. Technique comes third.

Before you decide what to tape, you need to know what you found.

**Step 1: Tissue Integrity Assessment**

Start with the foundation: Is the tissue actually damaged? Use movement testing, palpation, special tests. For an ankle: passive inversion/eversion (does it feel stable?), anterior drawer test (is the ligament torn?), weight-bearing tolerance (how confident is the patient?).

What you're looking for: Is this a tissue damage problem or a movement/confidence problem?

Example: Your ankle patient. You find intact ligaments, good ROM, but pain with weight-bearing. **Clinical finding:** Tissue is probably fine. The problem is confidence in that tissue and movement quality.

**Step 2: Movement Quality Assessment**

Now look at how they move. Not just "can they move?" but "how are they moving?"

For the ankle: Watch them walk. Watch them stand on one leg. Watch them weight shift. What you're observing: Is their nervous system protecting them from a real threat, or from a perceived threat?

**Example finding:** Your patient walks through slight inversion. Their foot never achieves neutral weight-bearing. Their nervous system is still protecting the ankle, even though the tissue is fine.

**Step 3: Functional Limitation Assessment**

Connect the assessment to the actual limitation: What can't they do? What are they avoiding? Why?

**Example:** Your patient avoids weight-bearing on the ankle. Not because of tissue damage, but because they've lost confidence in the ankle's stability. Their nervous system is saying "that ankle might give way again."

**Now your reasoning becomes clear:**

*Tissue is intact. Movement pattern is wrong. Confidence is low. Solution: tape that improves movement quality and cues confidence.*

This is completely different from "tape for ankle stability."

**The tape you choose now:**

- Improves proprioceptive feedback (helps the brain sense the ankle position better)
- Cues correct movement path (neutral weight-bearing instead of inversion compensation)
- Provides confidence without artificial bracing

Same ankle. Same patient. Completely different tape application. And now you know *why* that tape is the right choice. You reasoned your way to it.

---

## Why It Works

This approach works because it honors what we know about how pain and movement actually function.

Pain isn't always tissue damage. Sometimes pain is the nervous system's protective strategy. The ligament is fine, but the brain doesn't know that yet. The tape's job is to give the brain confidence.

Stability tape addresses tissue protection. But if there's no tissue damage, protection isn't the goal. Re-education is.

The research backs this. Studies show that structured assessment—determining what's actually wrong—predicts outcome better than condition-based taping protocols. When clinicians assess and reason, outcomes improve. When they apply protocols without reasoning, results are inconsistent.

Here's the biomechanics: taping can't replace damaged ligaments. But taping can improve proprioceptive feedback (the brain's sense of ankle position). Better feedback + better movement quality + better confidence = better function.

This progression—from assessment finding to clinical reasoning to technique choice—is how experts think. It's not complicated. It's just structured.

And here's what I wish someone had taught me twenty years ago: you don't need decades of experience to think this way. You need a repeatable assessment process and the willingness to let assessment data drive your decisions instead of diagnosis alone.

New graduates can think like this immediately. Experienced clinicians can learn to think this way tomorrow. The structure works regardless of experience level because it's not based on intuition. It's based on observable assessment findings.

---

## Clinical Takeaway

**Tape doesn't fail. Your reasoning does—if you skip it.**

The decision point is simple: before you decide what to tape, you must know what you found on assessment. Let the assessment data drive your reasoning. Let your reasoning drive your technique choice.

This means:
- Don't tape based on diagnosis name alone
- Don't assume you know what the problem is until you've assessed
- Don't apply the same taping protocol to every ankle sprain or shoulder impingement
- Do assess first; reason second; tape third

The patient with intact ligaments and movement compensation needs something different than the patient with tissue damage and instability. Your technique should reflect what you actually found, not what the diagnosis name suggests.

Remember this: clinical reasoning isn't a luxury. It's not something you develop after ten years of experience. It's a process you use from Day 1. Assessment → Finding → Reasoning → Technique.

That's how experts think. That's how you build confidence in your decisions. And that's how you get better results.
