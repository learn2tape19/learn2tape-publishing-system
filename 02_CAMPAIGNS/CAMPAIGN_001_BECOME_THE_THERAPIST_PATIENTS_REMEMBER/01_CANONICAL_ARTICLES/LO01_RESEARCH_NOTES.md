## Research Summary

### Core Belief Shift Being Taught

**Before:** Taping is a technique I apply to different body parts and conditions.

**After:** Taping is one expression of clinical reasoning. The reasoning drives the technique choice, not the other way around.

### Key Sources

#### 1. Ericsson on Expertise (Deliberate Practice)
- **Source:** Ericsson, A. K. (2008). "Deliberate Practice and the Acquisition and Maintenance of Expert Performance in Medicine and Related Domains." *Academic Medicine*, 83(10 Suppl), S52-S65.
- **Key Insight:** Expertise is built through deliberate practice and structured decision-making, not just years of experience
- **Application:** Explains why a 5-year clinician with structured reasoning can outthink a 20-year clinician without it
- **Quote:** "Expert performance is not due to some fixed innate talent but rather due to the effects of dedicated effort to improve performance in a specific domain."

#### 2. Kahneman & Tversky on Intuition vs. System
- **Source:** Kahneman, D., & Tversky, A. (1974). "Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." *Science*, 185(4157), 1124-1131.
- **Key Insight:** Intuition and quick judgment often mislead; structured processes reduce bias
- **Application:** Explains why clinical intuition alone is unreliable; assessment creates structure
- **Takeaway:** "Even the experienced observer is prone to overconfidence"

#### 3. Klein on Recognition-Primed Decision Making
- **Source:** Klein, G. (2015). "Seeing What Others Don't: The Remarkable Ways We Gain Insights." *PublicAffairs*.
- **Key Insight:** Expert intuition is based on pattern recognition from practice with feedback
- **Application:** Patterns only develop when clinicians reflect on what works and why
- **Teaching:** Explains how to build the "intuition" that actually works

## Clinical Examples

### Example 1: The Ankle Inversion (Primary Case)
**Presentation:** Patient twists ankle. Clear pain with weight-bearing. Assumes ligament damage.

**Common Mistake:** Tape for "ankle stability" — assume protecting the ligament is the goal.

**Better Approach:** Assess tissue integrity first. If ligament is intact, the goal is different: improve movement quality and weight-bearing confidence.

**Why It Matters:** Same ankle pain. Different assessment finding. Completely different taping goal. Clinical reasoning came before technique.

**Story Angle:** Patient came in convinced they had a torn ligament. Assessment showed otherwise. Patient was surprised. That surprise reveals the power of assessment-driven reasoning.

### Example 2: The Experienced Clinician Mistake
**Story:** Drew's early career experience where intuition led wrong. Years of experience assumed = good judgment, but reasoning wasn't solid.

**Teaching:** Experience without reflection doesn't improve; reflection with deliberate practice does.

**Insight:** This shows the difference between years-of-experience and deliberate-practice.

### Example 3: The New Graduate Who Got It Right
**Story:** A recent graduate with structured assessment thinking outthought an experienced clinician who relied on intuition.

**Teaching:** Reasoning matters more than experience.

**Why:** Validates that readers don't have to wait years to think like an expert.

## Teaching Framework

### The Core Principle
Taping is not technique first. Taping is clinical reasoning expressed through technique.

### The Teaching Progression
1. **Set up the problem:** Clinician has intuition but lacks confidence it's right
2. **Name the common mistake:** We tape based on condition name, not assessment finding
3. **Teach the better approach:** Let assessment drive the reasoning, let reasoning drive the technique
4. **Explain why:** Biomechanics + evidence + clinical experience
5. **Make it memorable:** "Reasoning before technique. Assessment before application."

### The Takeaway
Readers leave understanding that their clinical judgment is a learnable skill grounded in assessment, not a mysterious talent that takes 20 years to develop.

## Anticipated Reader Questions

**Q: Isn't experience the best teacher?**  
A: Experience teaches IF you reflect on it. Without reflection, experience just repeats.

**Q: How do I know if my reasoning is right?**  
A: Assessment findings should match your reasoning. If they don't, your reasoning needs refinement.

**Q: This sounds complicated. I just want to tape better.**  
A: This actually makes taping easier. You move from guessing to knowing.

**Q: How long until I think like an expert?**  
A: If you practice deliberately (with reflection), months. If you just repeat, years.
