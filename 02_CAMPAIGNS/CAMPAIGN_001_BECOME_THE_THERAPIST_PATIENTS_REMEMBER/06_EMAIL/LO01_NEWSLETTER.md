Hi [First Name],

You probably tape like most therapists do: ankle sprain → ankle stability tape. Shoulder impingement → shoulder support tape. Knee pain → knee support tape.

We learn patterns: diagnosis name = taping protocol. It's simple. It works... sometimes.

But here's what I discovered after 20 years: the diagnosis is just the beginning.

**The Real Question Comes After Assessment**

I had a patient with chronic ankle pain. For months, I'd been taping her ankle for "stability." Same technique every time. She'd feel better for a day or two, then the pain returned. We both got frustrated.

Then I actually *assessed* her movement—not just "can you move?" but *how are you moving?*

What I found: Her ligament was fine. Her movement pattern was wrong. She was compensating in subtle ways. All the stability tape in the world wouldn't fix that.

She needed movement tape, not stability tape. Tape that cued better movement patterns.

Same ankle. Different assessment finding. Completely different technique.

**Here's How Experts Think About It**

1. Assess: What did you actually find?
2. Reason: What does that finding mean?
3. Tape: Now you know why that tape is the right choice.

This isn't complicated. It's just structured.

And here's what I wish someone had taught me sooner: you don't need decades of experience to think this way. You need a repeatable assessment process and the willingness to let assessment data drive your decisions.

**Your Next Patient**

Before you tape, assess. Before you apply technique, reason.

The patient with intact ligaments and movement compensation needs something different than the patient with tissue damage and instability. Your technique should reflect what you actually found, not what the diagnosis name suggests.

Clinical reasoning isn't a luxury. It's how you build confidence in your decisions.

Drew
