## Headline

**Clinical Reasoning Before Technique: What Separates Expert Practitioners from Routine Ones**

---

## Article Body

In two decades of clinical practice and education, I've noticed a pattern that separates the practitioners who consistently get results from those who struggle to improve outcomes.

It's not experience. It's not credentials. It's not the latest techniques.

It's *how* they think about clinical decisions.

**The Standard Approach (And Why It Falls Short)**

Most practicing therapists operate from a diagnosis-driven model:

- Ankle sprain → stabilization tape
- Shoulder impingement → support tape
- Knee pain → restriction tape

The logic is straightforward: condition name predicts treatment protocol. Clinicians learn patterns, apply protocols, move to the next patient.

This model has an advantage: simplicity. A new graduate can follow a protocol without deep clinical understanding.

But it has a cost: inconsistent outcomes. Sometimes the standard protocol works beautifully. Sometimes it completely fails. And the clinician often doesn't know why.

**The Expert Model: Reasoning Before Technique**

Expert practitioners follow a different sequence:

1. **Assess first** — What does the clinical picture actually show?
2. **Reason second** — What does that assessment finding mean for this patient?
3. **Technique third** — Now select the intervention that addresses what you found.

The difference is subtle but profound.

I worked with a patient who had chronic ankle pain that conventional treatment couldn't resolve. The diagnosis was ankle sprain. By protocol, she needed ankle stabilization.

I'd been providing exactly that for months. Nothing changed.

When I performed a comprehensive movement assessment—not just "can you move?" but *how are you moving*—I found something unexpected: her ankle ligament was completely intact. But her movement pattern revealed nervous system protection. She was compensating subtly with each step, avoiding the proper weight-bearing pattern.

Stability tape wasn't the problem. The problem was that her nervous system needed re-education, not protection.

Different assessment finding. Different clinical reasoning. Different intervention. Better result.

**Why This Matters for Professional Development**

This distinction reshapes how we think about expertise:

The research is clear: structured clinical reasoning predicts outcome better than condition-based protocols alone. Clinicians who assess and reason get more consistent, better results.

But here's what many miss: you don't need twenty years to think this way.

Expertise based on intuition takes time to develop—and even then it's unreliable. Expertise based on systematic reasoning can develop much faster, because it's grounded in repeatable processes rather than accumulated pattern-matching.

A clinician with solid assessment skills and structured reasoning can outthink an experienced clinician operating on intuition.

**Building Clinical Authority Through Reasoned Practice**

For practitioners concerned about professional positioning: this is precisely where clinical authority lives.

Patients don't care how many years of experience you have. They care that you understand their problem and can explain why your treatment approach makes sense.

When you can assess thoroughly, reason clearly, and explain your thinking, you build a different kind of credibility. You're not just applying technique—you're thinking clinically about *their specific situation*.

This difference is visible to other professionals too. Physicians, physical therapists, and practice owners notice and respect clinicians who reason through their treatment decisions rather than follow protocols.

**The Path Forward**

If you're looking to deepen your clinical practice or sharpen your professional positioning:

The foundation is assessment plus structured reasoning.

1. Invest in assessment competency—the specific skills to find what's actually going on with each patient
2. Develop a repeatable decision-making framework—so reasoning becomes automatic, not an extra step
3. Practice explaining your thinking—so your reasoning becomes visible to patients and peers

This isn't about learning new techniques. It's about thinking differently about the techniques you already know.

The best insight of my career came from realizing that my clinical outcomes improved dramatically not when I learned new methods, but when I started reasoning systematically about which methods matched which clinical findings.

That shift is available to every practitioner willing to make it.
