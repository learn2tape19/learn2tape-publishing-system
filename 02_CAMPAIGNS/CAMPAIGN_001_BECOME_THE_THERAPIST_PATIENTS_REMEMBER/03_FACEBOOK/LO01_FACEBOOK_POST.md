## Post Copy

You probably tape like most therapists do.

Ankle sprain? Ankle stability tape. Shoulder impingement? Shoulder support. Knee pain? Knee brace tape.

Diagnosis name = taping protocol. Apply technique. Move to next patient.

Here's what I realized after two decades of taping: the diagnosis is just the starting point. The real thinking comes *after* you assess.

---

I had a patient with ankle pain that never got better. For months, I kept taping her ankle the same way—stability tape, support, protection. She'd feel better for a day, then pain returned.

We were both frustrated.

Then I actually assessed her movement. Not just "can you move?" but *how are you moving?*

What I found changed everything: her ligament was fine. No tissue damage. But her movement pattern was compensating. Her nervous system was still protecting an ankle that didn't need protecting.

Stability tape wasn't the answer. She needed movement tape—tape that helped her nervous system relearn the right pattern.

Same ankle. Different assessment. Different reasoning. Different technique. Better result.

---

**Here's how experts think about taping:**

1. ASSESS — What did you actually find?
2. REASON — What does that finding mean?
3. TAPE — Now you know why that choice is right.

This isn't complicated. It's structured thinking.

And here's what matters: you don't need 20 years of experience to think this way. You need assessment skills and the willingness to let data drive your decisions instead of diagnosis alone.

New graduates can think like this immediately. Experienced clinicians can learn this tomorrow.

Then try it with your next patient. Notice what happens when you let assessment drive your reasoning.

Clinical reasoning isn't a luxury. It's the foundation of everything else.
